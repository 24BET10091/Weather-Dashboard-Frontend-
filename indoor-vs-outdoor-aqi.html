Indoor AQI vs Outdoor AQI

Outdoor AQI reflects pollution around the building, but indoor air quality can be very different because buildings trap pollutants, ventilation changes, and indoor sources such as cooking, cleaning, smoking, and household products add pollution that outdoor readings do not capture.

What research shows

  • WHO and EPA guidance both note that indoor air can contain elevated fine particles and gases compared with outdoor air when ventilation is poor.
  • Studies show that cooking, cleaning, incense, candles, and smoking can sharply increase PM2.5 and VOC levels indoors.
  • Indoor pollutant levels often rise during occupancy and can stay high even when outdoor AQI is moderate or good.

When indoor AQI may be higher

  • During cooking and cleaning, especially without proper exhaust ventilation.
  • In homes or classrooms with low air exchange and sealed windows.
  • Near combustion sources such as candles, incense, tobacco smoke, or gas stoves.

Why outdoor AQI is not enough

Outdoor AQI is useful for the environment outside the building, but it does not measure indoor generation and accumulation. A home may have elevated PM2.5 or VOCs even when the outdoor index is labeled “good.”

Practical takeaways

  • Ventilate kitchens and bathrooms when possible.
  • Use an air purifier in rooms with persistent dust or smoke.
  • Monitor indoor air more closely if children, elderly adults, or people with asthma live in the home.
